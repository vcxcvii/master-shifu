# SWOT Analysis

## When to Use
- Strategic planning — where does the business stand today?
- Competitive assessment — what's our position vs competitors?
- New market entry or new product launch evaluation
- Pre-cursor to strategy formulation (SWOT feeds directly into strategic choices)
- Post-mortem on a failed initiative — what went wrong?
- M&A target evaluation — what are we buying (strengths) and inheriting (weaknesses)?

## Framework Structure

```
                     Internal (controllable)     External (uncontrollable)
                  ┌──────────────────────────┬──────────────────────────┐
                  │                          │                          │
     Positive     │    STRENGTHS (S)         │   OPPORTUNITIES (O)     │
     (helpful)    │    What we do well        │   What we can exploit    │
                  │                          │                          │
                  │  • Unique capabilities   │  • Market trends         │
                  │  • Valuable resources    │  • Customer needs        │
                  │  • Brand, IP, data       │  • Technology shifts     │
                  │  • Cost advantage        │  • Regulatory change     │
                  │  • Customer loyalty      │  • Competitor weakness   │
                  ├──────────────────────────┼──────────────────────────┤
                  │                          │                          │
     Negative     │    WEAKNESSES (W)        │    THREATS (T)           │
     (harmful)    │    Where we're vulnerable │   External risks         │
                  │                          │                          │
                  │  • Capability gaps       │  • Competitor moves      │
                  │  • Resource constraints  │  • Substitute products   │
                  │  • Cost disadvantage     │  • Regulatory threat     │
                  │  • Brand perception      │  • Economic downturn     │
                  │  • Operational issues    │  • Supply chain risk     │
                  └──────────────────────────┴──────────────────────────┘
```

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1. **Internal analysis: Identify STRENGTHS and WEAKNESSES**

   *Strengths* — What does the company do better than competitors?
   - Resources: capital, talent, technology, IP, brand equity, distribution network, customer data, patents
   - Capabilities: R&D speed, operational excellence, customer service, supply chain, marketing
   - Ask: "What would competitors say is our biggest advantage?"

   *Weaknesses* — Where is the company at a disadvantage?
   - Gaps: missing capabilities, outdated technology, high cost structure, weak brand in certain segments
   - Constraints: limited capital, talent shortage, regulatory exposure, concentration risk (one customer, one product)
   - Ask: "If we could fix one thing about this company, what would it be?"

   **Critical rule**: Strengths and weaknesses are relative to competitors, not absolute. A "strong brand" means nothing if all competitors also have strong brands.

2. **External analysis: Identify OPPORTUNITIES and THREATS**

   *Opportunities* — External trends the company can exploit:
   - Market growth, underserved segments, technology shifts, regulatory tailwinds
   - Customer needs not being met, adjacent markets, partnership possibilities
   - Competitor weakness or distraction

   *Threats* — External factors that could harm the business:
   - New or existing competitors, substitute products, changing customer preferences
   - Regulatory changes, economic downturn, supply chain disruption, technology disruption
   - Concentration risk in supply or customer base

3. **Pairing analysis (the most important step!)** — Connect quadrants for actionable strategy:

   | Pair | Strategy | Question |
   |---|---|---|
   | **S×O** | Offensive | Use strengths to capture opportunities |
   | **W×O** | Improvement | Fix weaknesses to capture opportunities |
   | **S×T** | Defensive | Use strengths to mitigate threats |
   | **W×T** | Survival/Minimize | Reduce exposure, exit, contingency plan |

4. **Prioritize** — Focus on 2-3 most impactful items per quadrant. A SWOT with 20 items per box is useless.

5. **Generate strategic options** — Based on the pairing analysis, what 3-5 specific actions should the company take? Connect each action back to the SWOT.

## Example

**Netflix SWOT (circa 2022):**

| Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---|---|
| Global brand with 230M+ subscribers | High content debt (~$15B) |
| Massive content library + viewing data | Content cost inflation (streaming wars) |
| Best-in-class recommendation algorithm | Password sharing (before crackdown) |
| Original content capability (Stranger Things, etc.) | Thin margins in mature markets |
| Global distribution platform | No live sports (high engagement category) |

| Opportunities | Threats |
|---|---|
| International expansion (Asia, Africa — still low penetration) | Disney+, HBO Max, Apple TV+ (fragmentation) |
| Gaming vertical (mobile games via Netflix) | Content cost arms race |
| Advertising tier (new revenue stream, higher ARPU) | Password sharing impact on subscriber numbers |
| Interactive content / live events | Cord-cutting slowing in US (market maturing) |

**Pairing analysis:**
- *S×O*: Use content data & global platform → fuel international expansion with locally relevant content
- *W×O*: Fix thin margins → launch ad tier (higher ARPU) to fund content investment
- *S×T*: Use recommendation algorithm → improve retention even as competitors fragment the market
- *W×T*: Address content debt → invest in owned IP (less reliance on licensed content) before cost crisis hits

## Common Pitfalls
- Laundry list without prioritization — not all strengths are equally important; rank them
- Confusing internal and external factors — "bad management" is a weakness (internal), not a threat (external)
- Skipping the pairing analysis — SWOT alone has no "so what"; the pairing is where strategy happens
- Listing generic items without specifics — "strong brand" needs: "strong brand in what, with whom, vs whom?"
- No competitive context — a strength that everyone has is not a strength
- Treating SWOT as an annual exercise instead of a dynamic tool for current decisions
- Forgetting that a strength in one context can be a weakness in another (e.g., strong brand in luxury = weakness in discount)
