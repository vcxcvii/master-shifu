## When to Use

The client needs to reduce costs or improve margins, and the problem is specifically on the cost side (not revenue). Use when profitability is driven by cost issues, margins are compressing, or the client needs to restructure to compete. Differentiate from general profitability: this one assumes revenue is fine, costs are the problem.

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1. **Segment the cost base**: Where is the money going?
   - Fixed vs variable: variable costs scale with activity; fixed costs need volume or restructuring to change
   - By department: manufacturing, sales, R&D, G&A — where are the largest buckets?
   - By value chain stage: raw materials → manufacturing → logistics → sales → after-sales
   - By product/SKU: P&L by product to identify loss-making lines (may need SKU rationalization)
   - By customer/channel: Not all customers are profitable. Fire the bottom 10%.

2. **Do value chain analysis**: For each stage in the value chain:
   - What is the cost?
   - What value does it create?
   - Could it be done cheaper by someone else (outsource)?
   - Could it be eliminated entirely?
   - Benchmark against industry best-in-class for each stage.

3. **Identify cost drivers**: What's causing the cost?
   - Labor: wages, headcount, productivity, overtime
   - Materials: raw material costs, waste/scrap, supplier margins
   - Overhead: rent, utilities, IT, corporate allocations
   - Logistics: transport, warehousing, fuel
   - Complexity costs: SKU count, customization, slow-moving inventory
   - Quality costs: rework, returns, warranty claims

4. **Develop cost reduction levers**:
   - **Quick wins** (3-6 months, low implementation cost):
     - Cut discretionary spending (travel, entertainment, consulting)
     - Hiring freeze / attrition management
     - Renegotiate supplier contracts
     - Reduce inventory levels
     - Eliminate waste (lean, Six Sigma)
     - Consolidate vendors for better pricing
   - **Structural changes** (6-24 months, higher investment):
     - Process reengineering (how work gets done)
     - Automation (robotics, RPA, software)
     - Organizational delayering (flatten management)
     - Footprint consolidation (close facilities)
     - Outsourcing/offshoring (non-core activities)
     - SKU rationalization (cut low-volume, low-margin products)
     - Zero-based budgeting (build budget from zero, not from last year + X%)

5. **Estimate impact and timeline**: For each lever, quantify the cost savings and one-time implementation cost. Prioritize by impact ÷ effort.

6. **Risk assessment**: Cost reductions can hurt growth, morale, quality, and brand. Flag risks and mitigation.

## Example: Manufacturer with Eroding Margins

**Situation**: Midwest industrial manufacturer with $500M revenue, margins declining from 12% to 6% over 3 years. Revenue is stable.

**Cost segmentation**: COGS 60% of revenue, SG&A 25%, R&D 8%, other 7%. Breakdown shows logistics at 12% of revenue (industry average is 6%).

**Value chain analysis**:
- Raw materials: On par with competitors
- Manufacturing: Labor costs 8% above regional average (union plant)
- Distribution/Logistics: 2x industry average. Root cause: 14 regional warehouses instead of 3 centralized DCs, and a poorly optimized delivery network (25% empty backhauls)
- Sales: 40-person sales force, 30% below industry in rev/salesperson

**Recommendations**:
- Quick win: Consolidate warehouses from 14 to 6 (saves $8M/year, $3M implementation)
- Quick win: Optimize delivery routing to fill backhauls (saves $4M/year)
- Structural: Consolidate to 3 DCs over 2 years (saves $12M/year total)
- Structural: Implement warehouse management system (saves $3M/year)
- Total savings: ~$27M/year, restoring margins from 6% to ~11%

## Common Pitfalls

- **Across-the-board cuts**: Cutting every department by 10% is lazy and dangerous. It starves growth areas while letting fat remain in others. Target cuts by value-add.
- **Firing "people" before fixing processes**: If the process is broken, firing people and hiring replacements doesn't fix it. Fix the work system first, then rightsize headcount.
- **Ignoring implementation cost**: Reorganizations, severance, system changes all cost money. Net savings = gross savings − implementation cost.
- **Cutting muscle, not fat**: Reducing sales headcount in a growth phase, cutting R&D that drives future revenue, reducing customer service that drives retention — these harm long-term value.
- **Treating costs as fixed that aren't**: Many "fixed" costs can be restructured — rent (sublease), IT (cloud), benefits (redesign).
- **Ignoring behavioral impact**: Cost cutting creates fear. Fear reduces innovation, risk-taking, and retention. Communicate the rationale and plan.
