# /outsourcing

## When to Use

Use this framework when the client must decide:
- Build a component internally vs. buy from a supplier
- Keep a function in-house vs. outsource (IT, manufacturing, customer service, logistics)
- Develop technology internally vs. license or acquire
- Vertically integrate (forward or backward) vs. stay at current stage
- Partner/JV vs. go solo

**Triggers**: "should we build or buy", "should we outsource", "we're considering vertical integration", "should we partner or go alone".

### Step 1: Define the Decision

Precisely scope what's being evaluated:
- **What**: Which product, component, service, or function is in question?
- **Current state**: Is it currently done in-house? Outsourced? New?
- **Options**: Build from scratch? Buy off-the-shelf? License? OEM? Joint venture? Partial outsource?
- **Decision criteria up front**: What matters most—cost, quality, speed, control, innovation, flexibility?

### Step 2: Strategic Analysis

**Core vs. Non-Core**
- Is this activity central to the client's competitive advantage?
- Does it involve proprietary knowledge or IP?
- Does it differentiate the client in the market?
- Rule: Outsource non-core activities; protect and invest in core activities.

**Control & Quality**
- How critical is quality consistency?
- Can quality be specified in a contract?
- Can you monitor supplier performance?
- If quality is hard to measure or enforce, insourcing is safer.

**Risk & Dependence**
- Single supplier risk: what if they fail, raise prices, or are acquired?
- IP risk: will the supplier learn your secrets and compete?
- Switching costs: how hard is it to change suppliers?
- Reputational risk: will the supplier's actions affect your brand?

**Flexibility & Speed**
- How fast does demand change?
- Does the client need the ability to scale quickly?
- Outsourcing offers variable cost flexibility; insourcing ties you to fixed costs.
- For volatile demand, outsourcing reduces risk of idle capacity.

### Step 3: Economic Analysis

**Total Cost Comparison**
- Make costs: raw materials + direct labor + manufacturing overhead + tooling/equipment + quality control + inventory carrying + management overhead
- Buy costs: purchase price + transportation + duties/tariffs + inspection + contract management + switching costs
- Include transaction costs: search, negotiation, enforcement, coordination

**Break-Even Analysis**
- Fixed costs of making: equipment, facilities, training, process setup
- Variable costs of making: labor, materials, energy per unit
- Purchase price per unit (all-in, delivered)
- Break-even volume = Make Fixed Costs / (Buy Price − Make Variable Cost per Unit)
- Above break-even: making is cheaper. Below break-even: buying is cheaper.

**Scale & Learning Effects**
- Does the client have scale to be cost-competitive?
- Will the supplier have better learning-curve economics?
- Can the client consolidate volume across multiple lines?

### Step 4: Capability & Market Analysis

**Internal Capability Assessment**
- Does the client have the skills, equipment, and experience?
- What would it take to build the capability?
- Are there competing internal priorities for capital and talent?

**Supplier Market Assessment**
- How many qualified suppliers exist?
- What is their reliability, financial health, and quality track record?
- Is the supplier market competitive or concentrated?
- Can the client negotiate favorable terms?

### Step 5: Hybrid Options & Recommendation

**Partial Solutions**
- Tapered integration: make some, buy some (benchmarking, risk mitigation)
- Phased approach: start with outsourcing, insource later when volume justifies
- Partnership / JV: share investment and risk
- Co-location: supplier operates on your site

**Decision Matrix**
| Criteria | Make | Buy | Partner |
|----------|------|-----|---------|
| Strategic fit (core) | + | − | ~ |
| Cost at current volume | $ | $ | $$ |
| Control | + | − | ~ |
| Risk | Market risk | Supplier risk | Shared risk |
| Flexibility | − | + | ~ |
| Speed to market | − | + | + |

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

### Step 1: Scope the Activity
Pin down exactly what's being considered. Ask: "What would we need to build internally? What does the supplier provide?" Get current volumes and growth projections.

### Step 2: Determine Strategic Importance
Is it core to the client's competitive advantage? Can outsourcing hurt their differentiation? If it's a core competency, lean toward making.

### Step 3: Calculate Full Cost of Each Option
Get the numbers: setup costs, per-unit costs, and expected volume. Compute break-even. Sensitivity: what happens if volume is 20% lower/higher than expected?

### Step 4: Assess the Supplier Market
How many suppliers? Are they reliable? What is the risk of price increases? Can the client negotiate long-term contracts?

### Step 5: Evaluate Risk
What happens if the supplier fails? What if quality drops? What if demand drops and the client has committed to a supplier contract? What if technology changes?

### Step 6: Recommend
Should they make, buy, or partner? Why? Include implementation plan: timelines, transition approach, key milestones.

## Example

**Scenario**: An electronics company needs a new battery component for their next-gen device. They expect 500k units in year 1, growing to 2M by year 3.

**Step 1 – Define**: Battery pack assembly. Currently buying from a single supplier in China at $45/unit.

**Step 2 – Strategic**: Battery is not core to their brand (customers buy for the software, not the battery). However, battery life is a key product spec. Quality control is critical. Score: moderate strategic importance.

**Step 3 – Economics**: Making in-house: $5M in equipment + $32/unit variable cost. Break-even: 5M / (45 − 32) = 384,615 units. At 500K units, making saves $1.5M/year. At 2M units, saves $21M/year. Strong economic case for making.

**Step 4 – Supplier market**: Many battery suppliers globally, but few with high-quality certification. Moderate switching costs. Price is negotiable with volume commitment.

**Step 5 – Risk**: Single-supplier dependency is a risk. But making requires $5M investment and new expertise. Volume might not materialize. Recommendation: Tapered integration—continue buying from supplier for year 1 (renegotiate price to $40 with higher volume), then insource in year 2 once volume is confirmed.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|---------|---------------|------------------|
| Only comparing unit costs | Ignores strategic value, risk, flexibility, and transaction costs | Use total cost of ownership, not just price |
| Forgetting transition costs | Switching suppliers or building capability has one-time costs | Include setup, training, testing, and ramp-up costs |
| Overestimating control benefits | "We'll make it ourselves to have control" is expensive and constraining | Outsourcing with good contracts can give sufficient control |
| Underestimating supplier risk | Single-supplier dependency is dangerous | Plan for supplier failure: multi-source or have backup |
| Ignoring core vs. non-core | Outsourcing a competitive advantage is dangerous | Protect capability that differentiates the business |
| Static volume assumption | Break-even depends on actual volume | Run sensitivity on volume (+/− 20%, 50%) |
| Overlooking supplier's margins | A supplier with thin margins may not invest or be reliable | Check supplier financial health and strategic fit |
| Treating it as binary | Make or buy is a spectrum | Consider hybrid: partial, phased, or partnered |
