## When to Use

The client is considering acquiring another company, merging with a competitor/supplier/customer, or deciding between building capabilities organically vs buying them. The core question: "Should we acquire this company? At what price? How do we make it work?"

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1. **Run Business Situation Framework for Company A (client)**: Understand the client's capabilities, strengths, weaknesses, strategy.

2. **Run Business Situation for Company B (target)**: What does B bring? Customers? Technology? Talent? Distribution? Brand? Manufacturing capacity?

3. **Run Business Situation for A+B**: What does the combined entity look like? Where do they overlap? Where are they complementary?

4. **Identify strategic fit**:
   - **Fill a capability gap**: A has great sales but no product? B has great product but no sales?
   - **Speed to market**: Building in-house takes 3 years; acquiring takes 6 months
   - **Eliminate a competitor**: Reduce rivalry, increase pricing power
   - **Vertical integration**: Control supply chain or distribution
   - **Diversification**: Reduce dependence on one market (caution: often destroys value)

5. **Quantify synergies**:
   - **Revenue synergies**: Cross-selling A's products to B's customers (and vice versa), geographic expansion, bundling, access to new segments. Revenue synergies are harder to capture — be conservative (they typically achieve only 50-70% of what's projected).
   - **Cost synergies**: Overlapping HQ functions (HR, finance, IT), combined manufacturing, procurement scale, distribution network optimization, elimination of duplicate roles. Cost synergies are more predictable.

6. **One-way exploitation check**: If A and B are nearly identical (same customers, same products, different scale), the primary benefit is capacity — redirect to /capacity-expansion.

7. **Financial analysis**:
   - Purchase price and structure (cash vs stock vs earn-out)
   - Integration costs (one-time: IT, legal, severance, rebranding)
   - Payback period: Total investment ÷ Annual synergy savings
   - Accretion/dilution: Does the deal increase EPS in year 1-2?
   - Valuation methods: DCF of target, comparable transactions, precedent multiples

8. **Risk assessment**:
   - Regulatory approval (antitrust, foreign investment review)
   - Culture clash (biggest single cause of M&A failure)
   - Customer retention risk
   - Key employee retention
   - Integration complexity

## Example: Tech Company Acquiring AI Startup

**Situation**: Large enterprise software company ($5B revenue, on-prem CRM) wants to acquire an AI startup ($50M, NLP/chatbot technology) for $800M.

**Strategic fit**: Client lacks AI capabilities, losing enterprise RFPs to competitors with AI features. Building in-house would take 3+ years. Startup has state-of-the-art NLP and 40 PhD-level researchers.

**Synergies**:
- Revenue: Embed chatbot into existing CRM — increases ASP by 20%. Cross-sell to 10K existing enterprise clients. Upside: ~$400M/year incremental revenue by Year 3.
- Cost: Limited overlap (different office, different talent base). Small G&A consolidation.

**Financials**: $800M purchase price + $200M integration (retention bonuses, tech stack migration, sales training). Synergy target: $100M cost + $400M revenue = $500M/year. Payback: ~2 years. Accretive by Year 2.

**Risk**: Startup culture (flat, fast) vs enterprise (hierarchical, process-heavy). 60% of acqui-hires lose 50%+ of acquired talent within 2 years. Retention packages critical.

**Recommendation**: Acquire with $300M upfront + $500M earn-out tied to product milestones and talent retention. Appoint startup CEO as SVP of AI Products.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Overestimating synergies**: Revenue synergies are routinely overestimated by 50-100%. Use a haircut factor. Cost synergies are more reliable but still get overestimated.
- **Ignoring culture clash**: "Soft" factors kill more deals than "hard" ones. Undervalue integration complexity at your peril.
- **Paying for expected synergies**: The purchase price should reflect standalone value + a reasonable portion of synergies, not 100% of projected synergies.
- **Integration complexity**: Two systems, two teams, two processes. IT integration alone can take 18-24 months and cost 5-15% of deal value.
- **Customer attrition**: Acquisitions create uncertainty. Customers may defect to competitors. Competitors actively poach during the disruption period.
- **Ignoring regulatory risk**: Antitrust review, foreign ownership restrictions, industry-specific regulations can block or delay deals.
