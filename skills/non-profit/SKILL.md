# /non-profit

## When to Use

Use this framework when the client is:
- A non-profit organization seeking to expand impact or improve sustainability
- A government agency needing to improve service delivery or reduce costs
- A social enterprise balancing mission and financial viability
- A foundation evaluating grant-making strategy or program effectiveness
- Any mission-driven organization that doesn't measure success by profit alone

**Triggers**: "we're an NGO", "we're a non-profit", "government client", "social enterprise", "our mission is...", "we need funding".

### Step 1: Define the Mission & Objectives

**Articulate the Mission**
- What is the organization's core purpose?
- Who do they serve (beneficiaries, not customers)?
- What social/environmental problem do they solve?
- Does the mission have a clear theory of change?

**Identify Stakeholders**
- Beneficiaries (primary stakeholders)
- Donors and funders (who pays the bills)
- Board of directors (governance and oversight)
- Staff and volunteers (talent and operations)
- Partners and collaborators (amplify impact)
- Government and regulators (compliance and policy)

**Define Success Metrics**
- Outputs: activities completed (meals served, students taught, trees planted)
- Outcomes: changes achieved (nutrition improved, literacy increased, carbon sequestered)
- Impact: long-term systemic change (poverty reduced, climate stabilized)
- Efficiency: cost per beneficiary, cost per outcome
- Sustainability: funding diversity, reserve ratio, donor retention

### Step 2: Analyze the Funding & Resource Model

**Revenue Sources**
- Individual donations: recurring vs. one-time, average gift size, donor retention
- Grants: foundations, government, corporate. Duration, restrictions, renewal likelihood
- Earned revenue: fees for service, product sales, social enterprise ventures
- Events: fundraising events, galas, campaigns
- Endowment: investment income
- In-kind contributions: donated goods, pro-bono services

**Funding Health Metrics**
- Revenue concentration: % from top 3 sources (danger zone > 50%)
- Unrestricted vs. restricted funds (flexibility to allocate)
- Overhead ratio: admin + fundraising as % of total expenses
- Fundraising ROI: $ raised per $ spent on fundraising
- Reserve ratio: months of operating expenses in reserve

**Cost Structure**
- Program costs: direct costs of delivering the mission
- Administrative costs: overhead, rent, management
- Fundraising costs: events, campaigns, donor management
- Full cost recovery: are programs priced (or grants sized) to cover their full costs?

### Step 3: Evaluate Program Effectiveness

**Impact Assessment**
- Is there a clear theory of change: inputs → activities → outputs → outcomes → impact?
- Is impact measured rigorously? (RCTs, pre/post comparison, qualitative evidence)
- What is the cost per unit of impact? (Cost per child educated, cost per life saved)
- Which programs have the highest impact per dollar?

**Operational Efficiency**
- Are programs delivered at scale or pilot stage?
- Is there duplication or overlap across programs?
- Can operations be streamlined without sacrificing quality?

**Scale Potential**
- Can the program be replicated in other geographies or populations?
- What are the barriers to scale: funding, talent, infrastructure, policy?
- Is the model franchiseable or teachable to partners?

### Step 4: Explore Strategic Options

**Fundraising Strategy**
- Strengthen individual giving: monthly giving programs, major donor cultivation
- Diversify funding sources: reduce dependence on any single donor or grant type
- Earned revenue: launch or expand fee-for-service, social enterprise, or licensing
- Impact investing: attract mission-aligned investment capital

**Partnerships & Collaboration**
- Partner with government for scale and funding
- Collaborate with other non-profits to reduce duplication and share overhead
- Corporate partnerships: cause marketing, employee giving, pro-bono support
- Academic partnerships for research and impact measurement

**Operational Improvements**
- Reduce overhead without starving mission: shared services, co-location, tech leverage
- Improve volunteer management: recruitment, training, retention
- Adopt technology: CRM for donor management, MIS for program tracking
- Merger or consolidation with similar organizations to achieve scale

**Mission Adjustment**
- Should the organization pivot to higher-impact activities?
- Should they narrow focus (deepen impact) or broaden (serve more populations)?
- Should they shift from direct service to advocacy or capacity building?

### Step 5: Recommend & Implement

**Prioritization**
- Criteria: impact potential, feasibility, cost, risk, alignment with mission
- Short-term quick wins: low-cost, high-impact changes
- Medium-term initiatives: require investment but strengthen sustainability
- Long-term transformation: strategic repositioning

**KPI Dashboard**
- Mission metrics: beneficiaries served, outcomes achieved, impact per dollar
- Financial health: revenue growth, funding diversity, reserve ratio
- Efficiency: cost per beneficiary, overhead ratio, fundraising ROI
- Organizational health: staff retention, volunteer hours, board engagement

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

### Step 1: Understand Mission and Funding Model
What do they do and for whom? Where does money come from and is it sustainable?

### Step 2: Assess Performance
How effectively do they deliver the mission? Impact per dollar vs. benchmarks.

### Step 3: Identify Gaps
Funding gap, impact gap (programs not working), efficiency gap (too much overhead), or scale gap (great model, small)?

### Step 4: Generate Options
3-5 options covering fundraising, partnerships, operational efficiency, or mission refocus.

### Step 5: Recommend and Build a Dashboard
Top priority with KPIs. Recommendations must respect mission, not just finances.

## Example

**Scenario**: Education non-profit provides after-school tutoring to 2,000 underprivileged students. 80% of funding from a single government grant renewing every 2 years.

**Step 1 – Mission**: Improve academic outcomes for low-income students. Theory of change: tutoring → better grades → higher graduation. Stakeholders: students, parents, school district, 30 staff, 200 volunteers.

**Step 2 – Funding**: 80% government grant (high concentration risk). 10% individual donations. 10% foundation grants. 3-month reserve (needs 6). Overhead at 25%.

**Step 3 – Impact**: Students score 15% higher on tests vs. control group. Cost per student: $1,200/yr. Program at 85% capacity.

**Step 4 – Options**:
1. Monthly giving program targeting alumni parents ($200k/yr new revenue)
2. Sliding-scale fees for families who can pay ($100k/yr)
3. Multi-year agreement with school district
4. Shared back-office with another non-profit

**Step 5 – Recommendation**: Prioritize funding diversification. Launch monthly giving (quick win) and negotiate multi-year district commitment. Reduce single-funding dependency from 80% to 50% within 2 years.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|---------|---------------|------------------|
| Treating it like a for-profit | Profit ≠ success; mission is the goal | Optimize for social ROI, not financial ROI |
| Ignoring the funding model | Without sustainable funding, mission fails | Always analyze both mission and money |
| Encouraging overhead cutting to zero | Some overhead is necessary for effectiveness | Right-size overhead; invest in infrastructure |
| Confusing outputs with outcomes | Counting activities ≠ measuring change | Measure outcomes and impact, not just activities |
| Forgetting who the customer is | Beneficiaries and funders are different stakeholders | Both need to be satisfied |
| Assuming all revenue is good | Restricted grants limit flexibility | Prioritize unrestricted funding |
| Recommending "just get more grants" | Grant funding is competitive and temporary | Diversify revenue across multiple types |
| Ignoring political context | Government and non-profit decisions are political | Consider stakeholder politics and public perception |
