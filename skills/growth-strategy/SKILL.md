# Growth Strategy / Ansoff Matrix

## When to Use
- Client wants to grow revenue or market share
- Evaluating expansion options (new products, new markets, or both)
- Portfolio review — which business units deserve investment?
- Post-M&A integration or turnaround growth planning
- Assessing whether organic growth, partnerships, or M&A is the right path
- Prioritizing growth initiatives when resources are constrained

## Framework Structure

                  ┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
  Existing        │                 │                 │

                  │  PENETRATION    │  DEVELOPMENT    │
                  │  (lowest risk)  │                 │
                  │                 │                 │

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1. **Map current position** — What products (or services) do you sell today? In which markets (geographies, customer segments, channels)? What is the revenue and margin contribution of each?

2. **Evaluate each quadrant starting from top-left:**

   **Market Penetration (lowest risk):**
   - Can we take share from competitors? (price, promotion, distribution)
   - Can we increase usage frequency among existing customers? (loyalty, bundling, upsell)
   - Can we reduce churn? (retention programs, improved service)
   - Is the market growing? If yes, holding share still counts as growth.
   - Typical levers: pricing, advertising, sales force, loyalty programs, service improvements, acquiring a competitor for share

   **Market Development:**
   - New geographies: domestic expansion, international entry (which countries? why now?)
   - New customer segments: adjacent demographics, B2C→B2B or vice versa
   - New channels: direct-to-consumer, retail partnerships, online, wholesale
   - Key question: do our products need adaptation for the new market? (localization, regulatory compliance, pricing)
   - Risks: cultural differences, regulatory hurdles, distribution complexity, brand dilution

   **Product Development:**
   - Adjacent products: what do our customers already buy that we don't offer?
   - New features: what would make our product 2x more valuable?
   - Platform expansion: can we build an ecosystem around our core product?
   - Innovation models: in-house R&D, partnerships, acquisitions, open innovation
   - Risk: canour organization execute on new product development? (different capabilities needed)

   **Diversification (highest risk):**
   - Related diversification: adjacent industry where core capabilities transfer (e.g., Apple from phones to wearables)
   - Unrelated diversification: pure financial play (e.g., GE entering media) — rarely works
   - Vertical integration: backward (acquire supplier) or forward (acquire distributor/retailer)
   - Key question: what unique advantage do we bring to the new space?
   - Risk: highest failure rate; management distraction; value destruction

3. **Evaluate each option:**
   - **Market attractiveness**: size, growth rate, margins, competitive intensity
   - **Competitive position**: can we win? do we have a right to win?
   - **Capabilities needed**: what must we build, buy, or borrow?
   - **Risk/return**: what's the downside? what's the upside if successful?

4. **Prioritize**: start from the top-left (least risky, most certain) and move right/down. Exhaust market penetration before diversifying. Most growth comes from the core.

5. **Build an execution roadmap**: which moves happen in year 1, 2, 3? How are they sequenced? What capabilities must be built first?

## Example

**Starbucks growth strategy (2010-2025):**
| Quadrant | Moves |
|---|---|
| *Market Penetration* | Opened more US stores (18K+), launched rewards program (30M+ members), mobile order & pay to increase frequency, targeted drive-thru expansion |
| *Market Development* | Entered China (now 6K+ stores, 2nd largest market), expanded aggressively in India (with Tata JV), opened in Vietnam, Indonesia |
| *Product Development* | Nitro cold brew, Refreshers, plant-based milks (oat, almond), food expansion (warm sandwiches, protein boxes), holiday seasonal innovations |
| *Diversification* | Reserve Roastery (experiential retail), RTD in grocery with PepsiCo, Siren retail brand, Teavana acquisition, alcohol in evening hours |

**Key insight**: ~70% of Starbucks growth came from penetration and market development (core business), with product development and diversification as smaller bets.

## Common Pitfalls
- Skipping penetration for the excitement of new markets (most growth should come from core)
- Diversifying into unrelated areas with no competitive advantage (conglomerate discount)
- Ignoring execution capacity — each quadrant requires different capabilities
- Treating the matrix as 4 independent strategies instead of a portfolio with interdependencies
- Forgetting that market development often requires product adaptation (localization is real)
- Assuming past growth strategy will work for future (market context changes)
- Not considering divestiture or harvest as a strategic option (not all business units need growth)
