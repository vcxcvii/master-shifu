# STP / Segmentation Targeting Positioning

## When to Use

STP (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning) is the foundational marketing strategy framework. It answers: "Who are our customers, which ones should we focus on, and how should they think about us?" Use when:

- "Who should our target customer be?"
- "How do we position our brand against competitors?"
- "We want to enter a new market — what's our go-to-market approach?"
- "Our marketing isn't working — are we targeting the right people?"
- Launching a new product, entering a new geography, repositioning a brand
- After market sizing in a case — STP adds depth to who the customers are

## Framework Structure

### Step 1: Segmentation — Divide the market into distinct groups

**Bases for segmentation:**

**Geographic:** Country, region, city, urban/rural, climate
**Demographic:** Age, gender, income, education, occupation, family size
**Psychographic:** Lifestyle, values, personality, social class, attitudes
**Behavioral:** Usage rate, purchase occasion, brand loyalty, benefits sought, readiness to buy

**Good segmentation criteria (usable segments must be):**
- **Identifiable:** Can we find and measure them?
- **Substantial:** Large or profitable enough to serve?
- **Accessible:** Can we reach them with marketing?
- **Differentiable:** Are they distinct from other segments?
- **Actionable:** Can we design a marketing mix for them?

### Step 2: Targeting — Evaluate and select segments

**Evaluation criteria:**
- Segment size and growth potential
- Segment attractiveness (margins, competition, barriers)
- Company objectives and resources
- Competitive position within the segment
- Strategic fit with overall direction

**Targeting strategies:**

| Strategy | Description | When to use |
|----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Undifferentiated (Mass)** | One offer for entire market | Homogeneous market, commodity product |
| **Differentiated** | Multiple offers for multiple segments | Diverse segments, strong capabilities |
| **Concentrated (Niche)** | One offer for one segment | Limited resources, specialized expertise |
| **Micro-marketing** | Customized for individuals/local | Segment-of-one, hyper-local |

### Step 3: Positioning — Define how you want to be perceived

**Positioning statement formula:**
> "To [target segment], [brand] is the [category] that [point of difference] because [reason to believe]."

**Positioning dimensions:**
- On attributes (e.g., "the safest car")
- On benefits (e.g., "helps you sleep better")
- On usage/application (e.g., "for post-workout recovery")
- On user (e.g., "for the creative professional")
- On competitor (e.g., "the anti-Apple")
- On category (e.g., "champagne of beers")

**Positioning map (Perceptual Map):**
- 2D plot of brands on key customer decision dimensions
- X-axis and Y-axis represent attributes customers care about (price vs. quality, convenience vs. variety, etc.)
- Reveals: Competitive clusters, gaps (uncontested space), and ideal position

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

### Step 1: Segment the market
- Identify 3-5 meaningful segments using relevant bases
- Choose segmentation bases relevant to the product (not all bases apply)
- Describe each segment in terms of size, needs, behavior
- Validate each segment against the five criteria (identifiable, substantial, accessible, differentiable, actionable)

### Step 2: Select target segment(s)
- Evaluate each segment on attractiveness and fit
- Recommend a targeting strategy (concentrated vs. differentiated vs. undifferentiated)
- Justify the choice — why this segment over others?
- Consider: Is the segment large enough? Can we win there? Do we have the capabilities?

### Step 3: Develop positioning
- Identify the segment's key decision criteria (what matters most to them?)
- Map the competitive landscape on those criteria (perceptual map)
- Look for an uncontested position or a better-positioned version of an existing position
- Craft the positioning statement

### Step 4: Align the marketing mix (4Ps/7Ps)
- Ensure product, price, place, and promotion all reinforce the chosen position
- Example: Premium position → premium product, premium price, selective distribution, brand advertising

## Example

**Context:** A health food company wants to launch a plant-based protein powder in India. The market is currently dominated by whey protein brands (MuscleBlaze, MyProtein). Develop STP.

**Segmentation:**

| Segment | Description | Size (est.) | Needs |
|---------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| Fitness enthusiasts | Gym-goers, 20-35, male-dominated | Large | Muscle gain, recovery, taste |
| Health-conscious women | 25-45, urban professionals | Medium | Weight management, clean ingredients |
| Elderly/nutrition seekers | 50+, health concerns | Small-medium | Digestibility, health maintenance |
| Vegans/plant-based | 25-40, ethical consumers | Small but growing | Plant-based, no animal products |
| Lifestyle beginners | 25-35, new to supplements | Medium | Easy to use, trusted brand, not intimidating |

**Targeting:**
- **Primary:** Health-conscious women + Lifestyle beginners — both underserved segments with high growth potential in plant-based
- **Secondary:** Vegans/plant-based core audience
- **Strategy:** Differentiated approach — core product for primary, variants for secondary
- **Rationale:** These segments want "clean, natural protein" which aligns with plant-based positioning. The whey giants don't serve them well.

**Positioning:**
- **Perceptual map axes:** Purity/Naturalness vs. Effectiveness
- **Current players:** MuscleBlaze (high effectiveness, moderate purity), MyProtein (high effectiveness, low purity)
- **Uncontested space:** High purity + high effectiveness — "protein that's good for you AND works"
- **Positioning statement:** "For health-conscious women and lifestyle beginners, NourishPlant is the plant-based protein powder that combines clinical effectiveness with clean, natural ingredients developed by nutritionists"

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Using demographics only** — "Women 25-45" is not a meaningful segment by itself. Combine with psychographic or behavioral bases. "Health-conscious urban women 25-45 who value clean labels" is better.

2. **Segments that don't meet the criteria** — A segment must be reachable and actionable. "People who like adventure" is a segment, but how do you reach them differently?

3. **Positioning that no one cares about** — The position must matter to the target segment. "The most technologically advanced" only works if the segment values technology.

4. **Targeting too many segments** — Beginners target 4-5 segments and position differently for each. This is expensive and dilutes the brand. Focus on 1-2 segments, especially in early stages.

5. **Ignoring competition** — Positioning is relative. You can't position in a vacuum. Always draw the perceptual map with competitors.

6. **Static positioning** — Customer preferences change. Competition moves. Repositioning is sometimes necessary (e.g., Old Spice from "grandpa's brand" to "young and edgy").

## Interview Tips

- Draw the perceptual map (2D grid with competitors plotted) — this is a powerful visual that shows sophisticated marketing thinking
- Use the positioning statement structure — it's precise and memorable
- For segmentation, always justify *why* you chose those bases
- When targeting, explicitly state what you're *not* targeting (shows strategic discipline)
- Combine with Marketing Mix (4Ps) for a complete go-to-market recommendation
- Good lead-in: "Before we decide *how* to market, let's first understand *who* we're marketing to"
