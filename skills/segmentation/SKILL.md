# /segmentation

## When to Use

Use this framework when the client needs to:
- Identify which customer groups to target
- Understand why certain customers behave differently
- Improve marketing ROI by focusing on the right segments
- Develop differentiated products or value propositions
- Find growth opportunities in underserved segments
- Diagnose why a product is underperforming with certain groups
- Prioritize customer acquisition or retention efforts

**Triggers**: "who should we target", "segment our customers", "we need to understand our customers better", "which customer group is most profitable", "our marketing isn't working".

### Step 1: Choose Segmentation Criteria

**Demographic (Who they are)**
- Age, gender, income, education, occupation
- Geographic: region, urban/rural, climate, density
- Household: size, lifecycle stage, marital status
- Firmographic (B2B): industry, company size, revenue, location, decision-maker role

**Psychographic (Who they are at the core)**
- Values and beliefs: what matters to them?
- Lifestyle: how do they live and spend their time?
- Personality traits: risk-taker vs. cautious, innovator vs. late-adopter
- Attitudes: toward the category, brands, price, quality, service

**Behavioral (What they do)**
- Usage: heavy, medium, light, non-user
- Purchase occasion: everyday, special occasion, impulse, planned
- Benefits sought: convenience, quality, price, status, service
- Loyalty status: loyal, switcher, price-sensitive, brand-agnostic
- Readiness stage: unaware, aware, interested, intending, purchasing
- Channel preference: online, in-store, mobile, B2B direct

**Needs-Based (Why they buy—most powerful)**
- What functional problem are they solving?
- What emotional need is being met?
- What are their unspoken or unmet needs?
- How do they define value?

**Best practice**: Use needs-based segmentation as the primary dimension. Supplement with demographic and behavioral for reachability.

### Step 2: Identify Distinct Segments

**Data Sources**
- Customer transaction data: purchase history, frequency, basket size
- CRM data: demographics, interactions, service history
- Survey data: attitudes, preferences, satisfaction
- Behavioral data: website/app analytics, clickstream
- Third-party data: syndicated research, census, panel data
- Qualitative research: interviews, focus groups, ethnography

**Analytical Approaches**
- RFM analysis (Recency, Frequency, Monetary value)
- Cluster analysis (K-means, hierarchical clustering)
- Factor analysis + cluster (for psychographic data)
- Persona development (qualitative synthesis into archetypes)

**What Makes a Good Segment?**
- Identifiable: can you find them?
- Substantial: large enough to matter?
- Accessible: can you reach them efficiently?
- Differentiable: do they respond differently to marketing?
- Actionable: can you create a tailored offering?
- Stable: will they stay similar over the planning horizon?

**Profile Each Segment**
- Name the segment (memorable descriptor)
- Size and revenue contribution
- Key demographics and psychographics
- Core needs and purchase criteria
- Current behavior: what they buy, where, how often, how much
- Relationship to the client: awareness, usage, satisfaction

### Step 3: Evaluate & Prioritize Segments

**Evaluation Criteria**

| Criterion | Question | Metric |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Size | How many customers? | # of customers, $ revenue |
| Growth | Is it expanding? | CAGR, population trend |
| Profitability | What margins? | Gross margin, CLV, CAC |
| Competitive position | Can we win here? | Share, strength vs. competitors |
| Strategic fit | Aligned with our capabilities? | Capability match score |
| Accessibility | Can we reach them? | Media reach, channel coverage |

**Prioritization Matrix**: Plot segments on Attractiveness vs. Competitive Strength. Primary targets = high on both.

**Financial Prioritization**: Prioritize segments with highest CLV:CAC ratio. Consider growth trajectory, not just current value.

### Step 4: Recommend Targeting & Positioning

**Targeting Strategy**
- **Undifferentiated**: one offering for the whole market (mass)
- **Differentiated**: distinct offerings for multiple segments
- **Concentrated**: focus on one or two segments (niche)
- **Micromarketing**: tailored to individuals or small groups

**Value Proposition Design**
For each targeted segment:
- What is their primary unmet need?
- What is our unique ability to meet it?
- What is the compelling reason to buy from us?
- How do we win vs. alternatives?
- Frame the value proposition: "For [target segment], [brand] is the [category] that [key benefit] because [reason to believe]."

**Go-to-Market Implications**
- Product: features, packaging, sizing, quality level
- Price: price point, willingness to pay, sensitivity
- Place: channels, distribution, sales model
- Promotion: messaging, media mix, creative, influencers

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

### Step 1: Define Purpose
Why segment? What decision will it inform? (Targeting, product design, pricing, marketing spend, retention)

### Step 2: Choose Criteria
2-3 dimensions. Best practice: needs-based + behavioral first. Add demographics for reachability.

### Step 3: Identify Segments
3-6 distinct segments. Name and profile each in 2-3 sentences.

### Step 4: Size and Rank
For each: customer count, revenue, growth, profitability, competitive strength. Rank by attractiveness.

### Step 5: Select Target
Choose 1-3 segments. Articulate why these.

### Step 6: Design the Offer
Tailored value proposition per segment. Changes in product, price, place, promotion.

## Example

**Scenario**: Mid-market hotel chain wants to improve marketing ROI. Currently targets "business travelers" broadly.

**Step 1 – Criteria**: Needs-based + behavioral (travel frequency). Demographics for reachability.

**Step 2 – Segments**:
1. **"Road Warriors"** (35%): 20+ nights/yr. Value consistency, speed, loyalty rewards. Price-sensitive within range. Aged 35-55.
2. **"Weekend Escapers"** (25%): 4-6 weekends/yr. Value experience, ambiance. Less price-sensitive. 25-45, couples, higher income.
3. **"Budget Conscious"** (20%): 2-3 trips/yr. Value lowest price. Extremely price-sensitive.
4. **"Event Attendees"** (20%): Conferences, weddings. Value convenience, group rates, flexible cancellation.

**Step 3 – Evaluation**: Road Warriors = high frequency, moderate margins, intense competition. Weekend Escapers = higher RevPAR, less competition. Budget Conscious = low revenue, no loyalty. Event Attendees = high volume in clusters, low margin.

**Step 4 – Targeting**: Prioritize Weekend Escapers (highest profit, underserved) and Road Warriors (loyalty potential). De-emphasize Budget Conscious.

**Step 5 – Positioning**: Road Warriors → "Your reliable home on the road" (consistency, loyalty program). Weekend Escapers → "Discover more than a room" (curated local experiences, upgraded rooms).

**Step 6 – Go-to-Market**: Road Warriors via LinkedIn, industry events, loyalty email. Weekend Escapers via Instagram, travel blogs, Google Ads.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|---------|---------------|------------------|
| Demographic-only segmentation | "Women 25-45" is not a segment—they have different needs | Segment by needs and behavior first, describe by demographics |
| Creating too many segments | Can't operationalize 15 segments. Each needs a different strategy | Limit to 3-6 actionable segments |
| Forgetting to quantify | A segment that can't be sized can't be prioritized | Estimate size, revenue, and profit for each |
| Treating all customers equally | Wastes resources on low-value segments | Differentiate investment by segment value |
| Picking a segment that can't be reached | If you can't find or reach them, strategy fails | Check accessibility alongside attractiveness |
| Static segmentation | Customer needs evolve | Refresh segmentation periodically (annually or biannually) |
| Ignoring segment cannibalization | A new offer for one segment might steal from another | Model interaction effects between segments |
| Segmentation without activation | A segmentation that sits in a drawer is worthless | Link segments to go-to-market decisions |
