## When to Use

The go-to framework for any case that doesn't neatly fit profitability, market entry, pricing, or other specialized frameworks. Use when the client says "we're losing share," "our business is struggling," "help us grow," or "assess our strategy." Also use as the opening structure for any case before diving into a specific branch.

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1. **Set the landscape**: Ask clarifying questions about the client, industry, time horizon, and what "success" looks like.

2. **Work the 4 quadrants systematically**:

   **Customer** — Who buys and why?
   - Segment the customer base (demographics, psychographics, firmographics for B2B)
   - What drives purchase decisions? Price? Quality? Brand? Convenience?
   - How price-sensitive are they? How loyal?
   - Are they concentrated (few big buyers) or fragmented?
   - How do they want to buy? Online? Retail? Direct sales?

   **Company** — What is the client good at?
   - Core capabilities and competitive advantages
   - Cost position vs competitors
   - Distribution reach and channel relationships
   - Brand equity with customers
   - Financial capacity (cash, debt capacity, margins)
   - Technology, patents, data advantages
   - Organizational strengths/weaknesses

   **Competition** — Who else serves these customers?
   - How concentrated is the market? (HHI index)
   - How do competitors behave? Rational? Aggressive? Cooperative?
   - What does best-in-class look like?
   - What are barriers to entry/exit?
   - Are competitors growing, shrinking, entering, exiting?

   **Product** — What is being sold?
   - Commodity (price-based competition) or differentiated (value-based)?
   - What substitutes exist? (direct + indirect)
   - What complements strengthen demand?
   - Where in the product lifecycle? Introduction → Growth → Maturity → Decline

3. **Identify the key leverage point**: One of the four quadrants is usually the primary driver of the problem. Don't spend equal time on all four — find the root cause quadrant and dive deep.

4. **Form hypotheses**: Based on the analysis, generate 2-3 hypotheses for what is happening and why.

5. **Test and recommend**: Evaluate each hypothesis with data (or request data), then recommend an action plan targeting the root cause.

## Example: Department Store Losing Share to Discounters

**Situation**: Regional department store chain with 40 stores seeing 15% annual share loss to discount retailers (TJ Maxx, Ross, online).

**Analysis**:
- **Customer**: Core customer is 45-65, mid-income, suburban. Younger customers are defecting to discounters and online. Price sensitivity increasing due to inflation.
- **Company**: Strong brand, prime real estate (owned, not leased), excellent customer service. Higher cost structure (labor, real estate carrying costs). Low digital capability — no omnichannel.
- **Competition**: Discount retailers have 20-30% lower prices. Online (Amazon) has convenience. Both have lower cost structures.
- **Product**: Mid-market apparel/housewares — largely undifferentiated from what discounters sell. Private label underperforms.

**Key leverage point**: Cost structure. Client can't win on price with current cost base. But owned real estate is an asset.

**Recommendation**: (1) Sublease excess retail space to generate revenue, (2) Build private label for margin, (3) Launch omnichannel (buy online/pick up in store) leveraging store-as-warehouse advantage, (4) Rightsize store footprint.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Spending equal time on all 4 quadrants**: The power of this framework is finding the *driver*. If competition is the problem, spend 10% on company internal analysis and 90% on competitive dynamics.
- **Skipping the "so what"**: Listing facts in each quadrant is table stakes. The insight comes from connecting them — e.g., "Customers are price-sensitive AND our product is a commodity AND our costs are 20% above the low-cost competitor."
- **Confusing description with diagnosis**: "Our costs are high" is description. "Our costs are high because our union labor agreement limits shift flexibility, causing 15% idle time" is diagnosis.
- **Forgetting external trends**: Regulatory changes, technology shifts, demographic trends, and macroeconomic conditions affect all 4 quadrants. Add a fifth column for "Context" if needed.
