# /due-diligence

## When to Use

Use this framework when the client needs to:
- Evaluate a potential acquisition target
- Assess an investment opportunity
- Conduct a pre-deal business review
- Identify risks in a potential transaction
- Validate assumptions before making an offer
- Screen multiple candidates for investment

**Triggers**: "we're considering acquiring X", "should we invest in this company", "we need to evaluate a target", "what are the risks of this deal".

### Step 1: Market & Strategic Diligence

**Industry Analysis**
- Market size, growth rate, and trends
- Industry structure: Porter's Five Forces
- Regulatory environment and tailwinds/headwinds
- Cyclicality and seasonality

**Competitive Position**
- Market share and trend (gaining or losing?)
- Differentiation and moat: brand, technology, scale, network effects, switching costs
- Customer concentration: top 5 customers as % of revenue
- Supplier concentration and dependencies

**Strategic Rationale**
- Why does the client want to acquire? (Scale, capabilities, geography, talent, eliminate competition?)
- Synergies: Revenue synergies (cross-sell, pricing) vs. Cost synergies (overlap elimination, scale)
- Are the synergies real and achievable? Quantify conservatively.

### Step 2: Financial Diligence

**Historical Performance**
- Revenue growth: organic vs. acquisition-driven
- Margin trends: gross, operating, net
- Cash flow conversion: FCF as % of EBITDA
- Working capital trends: DSO, DPO, inventory turns
- Capex requirements relative to depreciation

**Quality of Earnings**
- Normalize EBITDA: add back one-time items, owner perks, non-market salaries
- Recurring revenue %: what portion of revenue is predictable?
- Revenue recognition: are there aggressive accounting policies?
- Channel stuffing, bill-and-hold, or other red flags

**Balance Sheet Health**
- Debt levels and debt service capacity
- Off-balance-sheet liabilities: leases, pensions, guarantees
- Asset quality: is inventory obsolete? Are receivables collectible?

**Projections & Valuation**
- Are management projections realistic? Past accuracy?
- Key assumptions: growth rate, margin expansion, capex
- Valuation methods: DCF, comparable companies, precedent transactions
- Implied multiples: EV/EBITDA, P/E, EV/Revenue
- Sensitivity analysis: what if growth is 2% lower? Margins 100bps lower?

### Step 3: Operational Diligence

**Operations & Supply Chain**
- Facility condition, capacity utilization, age of equipment
- Supply chain resilience: single-source dependencies, geopolitical risk
- Quality metrics: defect rates, return rates, warranty costs
- IT systems: ERP, software stack, data infrastructure

**People & Organization**
- Key person dependency: would the business survive without the founder?
- Management team depth and quality
- Employee turnover, morale, and culture fit
- Labor relations: union contracts, wage pressures

**Technology & IP**
- Proprietary technology, patents, trade secrets
- Technology roadmap: is tech current or legacy?
- R&D spending as % of revenue and its effectiveness
- IT security: past breaches, vulnerabilities

### Step 4: Legal & Regulatory Diligence

**Legal Structure**
- Corporate structure: subsidiaries, joint ventures
- Ownership: cap table, shareholder agreements
- Board composition and governance

**Contracts & Commercial**
- Key customer contracts: duration, termination clauses, renewal risk
- Supplier agreements: exclusivity, minimum commitments
- Distribution and partnership agreements
- Non-compete and non-disclosure agreements

**Regulatory Compliance**
- Industry-specific regulations (FDA, FCC, SEC, etc.)
- Environmental compliance and liabilities
- Data privacy: GDPR, CCPA compliance
- Intellectual property: patents filed, pending, infringed
- Pending or threatened litigation

### Step 5: Risk Assessment & Synthesis

**Risk Matrix**
| Risk | Severity | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|------|----------|------------|------------|
| Customer concentration | High | Medium | Diversify or retain via contracts |
| Founder dependence | High | High | Retention package, build mgmt |
| Technology obsolescence | Medium | Medium | R&D roadmap, acquire tech |
| Regulatory change | High | Low | Monitor, legal counsel |

**Deal Breakers**: Fraud, unsustainable concentration, regulatory threat to business model, cultural incompatibility, valuation destroying value even with synergies.

**Final Recommendation**
- Buy at current valuation (with conditions)
- Buy only if price adjusts for specific risks
- Walk away (risks too high / price too high)
- Go to next stage: LOI, exclusivity, detailed diligence

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

### Step 1: Understand the Deal
Buyer strategy, target rationale, headline financials (revenue, EBITDA, growth).

### Step 2: Identify Key Risks
Customer concentration, technology risk, integration challenge. Focus on biggest risks first.

### Step 3: Verify the Numbers
Normalize EBITDA. Check revenue quality. Stress-test projections. Confirm working capital trends.

### Step 4: Assess the Team
Management competence and retention risk. Cultural compatibility is the #1 reason integrations fail.

### Step 5: Synthesize
3-5 key risks, mitigation options, price justification, conditions to change recommendation.

## Example

**Scenario**: PE firm evaluating acquiring a legal-tech SaaS company for 8x EBITDA ($120M EV on $15M EBITDA). Revenue $40M, growing 12%.

**Step 1 – Market**: Legal tech, growing 8%/yr. Target is #2 with 15% share behind competitor at 30%.

**Step 2 – Financial**: 37.5% EBITDA margin, 80% recurring revenue, 8% churn. DSO 45 days. Quality of earnings is clean. Projections of 15% growth are aggressive vs. market at 8%.

**Step 3 – Operational**: Founder/CEO runs sales (key-man risk). 25% of revenue from one customer. Modern tech stack. R&D at 20% of revenue.

**Step 4 – Legal**: Clean IP. No litigation. 30-day termination clauses in customer contracts (revenue risk).

**Step 5 – Synthesis**: Key risks are customer concentration and CEO dependency. Mitigation: retention package for CEO, sales team expansion. 8x is fair. Recommend with conditions: negotiate retention pre-close, build sales team to diversify revenue.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|---------|---------------|------------------|
| Over-relying on management projections | Managers are optimistic; incentives skew forecasts | Build independent projections; stress-test assumptions |
| Ignoring integration costs | Deals often fail in post-merger integration | Include integration cost and risk in valuation |
| Confusing EBITDA with cash flow | EBITDA ignores capex, working capital, and interest | Focus on free cash flow |
| Inadequate customer diligence | A few customers leaving post-acquisition can destroy value | Talk to customers; check contract terms; analyze churn |
| Underestimating culture clash | Cultural incompatibility kills synergies | Assess culture during diligence; plan integration |
| Confirmation bias | Falling in love with the deal | Appoint a devil's advocate; formally document risks |
| Neglecting working capital | A growing company consumes cash via working capital | Model working capital requirements in the cash flow |
| Overestimating synergies | Synergies are harder to achieve than assumed | Take 50% of estimated synergies; add 6 months to timeline |
