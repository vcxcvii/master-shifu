# New Product Introduction / Innovation Framework

## When to Use

The New Product Introduction (NPI) framework helps evaluate whether to develop and launch a new product. It covers the entire decision pipeline — from idea screening through commercialization. Use when:

- "Should we launch this new product?"
- "We have an idea for a product — how do we evaluate it?"
- "How should we bring this innovation to market?"
- "We're getting into [new category] — walk me through the launch plan"
- Evaluating an acquisition target's product pipeline
- Innovation strategy: incremental vs. breakthrough products
- Post-merger product portfolio integration

## Framework Structure

The NPI framework is organized in five phases, but in interviews we focus on three layers of analysis:

### Layer 1: Market Viability (Should we do this?)

**Customer Need:**
- Is there a real, unmet need? (not just a "nice to have")
- How big is the need? (market sizing)
- How frequently does the need occur?
- How urgent is the need? (willingness to pay)
- **Tool:** Jobs-to-be-done, problem interviews, surveys

**Market Attractiveness:**
- Market size and growth rate (TAM, SAM, SOM)
- Competitive landscape: Who else is solving this? How well?
- Market trends: Tailwinds or headwinds?
- Regulatory environment: Is there a path to approval?
- **Tool:** Porter's Five Forces, market sizing

**Value Proposition:**
- Does our product solve the need better than alternatives?
- What is the unique advantage? (faster, cheaper, better, easier)
- Can we articulate it in one sentence?
- **Tool:** Value proposition canvas, positioning statement

### Layer 2: Internal Feasibility (Can we do this?)

**Technical Feasibility:**
- Do we have the technology/IP to build this?
- What is the development timeline?
- What are the technical risks and unknowns?
- Can we protect the innovation? (patents, trade secrets)

**Operational Feasibility:**
- Can we manufacture/source at scale?
- Do we have the supply chain to deliver?
- What is the unit cost trajectory? (learning curve)
- Do we need new capabilities, partners, or facilities?

**Financial Feasibility:**
- Development cost and timeline to break-even
- Unit economics: COGS, margin, contribution
- Required investment vs. available capital
- ROI, NPV, IRR — does this meet our hurdle rate?
- **Break-even analysis:** How many units must we sell to break even?

### Layer 3: Go-to-Market Strategy (How do we launch?)

**Segmentation & Targeting:**
- Which customer segment to target first? (beachhead)
- Early adopters vs. mainstream — different strategies
- Geographic scope: Phased rollout or national launch?

**Marketing Mix (4Ps):**
- **Product:** Features, packaging, variants, quality level
- **Price:** Penetration vs. skimming vs. competitive pricing
- **Place:** Direct, retail, online, distributor, platform
- **Promotion:** Launch campaign, PR, sales channels, trade promotions

**Staging & Milestones:**
- Phase 1: Test/pilot market (validate demand)
- Phase 2: Regional rollout (optimize operations)
- Phase 3: National/global scale (maximize share)
- Each phase has clear go/no-go decision criteria

### The Decision: Go / No-Go / Pivot

| Assessment | Decision |
|------------|----------|
| Viable + Feasible + Strong GTM | **Go** — full launch |
| Viable + Not yet feasible | **Hold** — invest in feasibility first |
| Not viable + Feasible | **No-Go** — don't build what nobody wants |
| Viable + Feasible + Weak GTM | **Pivot** — fix go-to-market before launch |
| Not viable + Not feasible | **Kill** — move on |

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

### Step 1: Understand the product and the ask
- Clarify: Is this a completely new product, a line extension, or a repositioning?
- Clarify: Are we evaluating a specific idea, or generating options?
- Set success criteria early: "What would make this a success? Revenue target? Market share? Strategic value?"

### Step 2: Assess MARKET VIABILITY (start here — if no market, stop)
- Estimate market size (top-down and bottom-up)
- Define the target customer and their need
- Assess competition and current alternatives
- Validate the value proposition — is it meaningfully better?

### Step 3: Assess INTERNAL FEASIBILITY
- Can we build it? (technology, talent, IP)
- Can we make it? (manufacturing, supply chain, quality)
- Can we afford it? (investment required, unit economics, break-even)
- **If viable but not feasible:** Consider partnerships, licensing, or phased development

### Step 4: Develop GO-TO-MARKET strategy
- Identify the beachhead segment (who will adopt first?)
- Design the 4Ps for that segment
- Plan the rollout phases with stage-gate criteria

### Step 5: Make the recommendation
- Go / No-Go / Pivot with clear rationale
- If Go: Timeline, investment required, expected returns, key risks
- If No-Go: What would need to change for reconsideration?
- If Pivot: What specifically needs to change?

## Example

**Context:** A large Indian FMCG company, "FreshFoods," is evaluating launching a ready-to-eat (RTE) traditional Indian breakfast product. They already have a strong distribution network and brand in packaged foods. The product would be shelf-stable, microwaveable, and priced at Rs 50-80 per serving.

**Market Viability:**
- **Customer need:** Urban professionals (25-40) want a healthy, quick, hot breakfast. Currently eating packaged cereals (dissatisfied with taste), skipping breakfast, or eating roadside food (quality concerns). Need is real and growing.
- **Market size:** TAM = Rs 12,000 Cr (packaged breakfast in India). SAM = Rs 2,400 Cr (RTE Indian breakfast). Growing at 15% CAGR.
- **Competition:** Organized players (MTR, Gits) are available but limited in distribution. Unorganized (local tiffin services) dominate but have quality inconsistency. White space for a branded, widely available option.
- **Value proposition:** "Restaurant-quality traditional breakfast in 2 minutes — available at your local kirana store"
- **Market verdict:** ✔ Viable

**Internal Feasibility:**
- **Technical:** FreshFoods has R&D capability in shelf-stable foods (existing noodle, soup business). Can develop in 6 months.
- **Operational:** Existing manufacturing plants can be upgraded. Distribution network already covers 500,000+ retail outlets.
- **Financial:** Rs 30 Cr development cost. Rs 15 Cr for plant upgrade. Unit COGS = Rs 30. Wholesale price = Rs 45. Retail price = Rs 60. Contribution margin = Rs 15/unit. Break-even = 3 Cr units (approx. 2.5 years at projected volumes).
- **Feasibility verdict:** ✔ Feasible

**Go-to-Market:**
- **Beachhead:** Top 10 cities, targeting working couples and single professionals aged 25-40
- **Product:** 4 varieties initially (poha, upma, veg pulav, idli)
- **Price:** Rs 60 MRP (premium over Gits at Rs 45 but justified by quality + distribution)
- **Place:** Modern trade + e-commerce first, then kirana stores
- **Promotion:** TV+Digital campaign targeting morning routines, in-store sampling at modern trade
- **Phases:** City-level pilot → Regional rollout → National

**Recommendation: GO**
Launch with Rs 45 Cr total investment. Target Rs 100 Cr revenue in Year 3. Key risks: consumer adoption of RTE breakfast (pilot phase mitigates), shelf-life management.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Skipping market viability** — Many candidates jump straight to "can we build it?" without first asking "does anyone want it?" The #1 reason new products fail is no market need. Assess viability FIRST.

2. **Overly optimistic market sizing** — TAM is not SAM is not SOM. Just because the overall breakfast market is Rs 12,000 Cr doesn't mean you'll capture it. Be realistic about what's addressable.

3. **Ignoring substitutes** — A new product competes with current behavior. Even if there's no direct competitor, your competition is "customer continuing current habit." Ask: what are they doing today instead?

4. **Confusing product features with benefits** — A new feature is not a value proposition. "30% faster cooking" is a feature. "Get your morning back" is a benefit. Position on benefits.

5. **Neglecting the execution gap** — A great product fails without distribution, sales capability, and after-sales support. Just because you *can* build it doesn't mean you *can* sell it.

6. **Not defining success criteria upfront** — "Success" is vague without specific targets. Define: Revenue at Year 1/3/5, market share, payback period, strategic value.

7. **One-size-fits-all launch** — Not all customers adopt at the same time. Use the technology adoption lifecycle (innovators → early adopters → early majority → late majority → laggards). Tailor the launch to the adoption stage.

8. **No phase-gates** — Committing full capital upfront is risky. Recommend a phased approach with go/no-go decisions after each phase.

## Interview Tips

- Lead with **market viability** — it shows customer-centric thinking
- The **beachhead strategy** (start narrow, prove concept, expand) is a hallmark of strong NPI analysis
- Use the **technology adoption lifecycle** to recommend who to target first and how the marketing mix changes as you move across segments
- Show judgment: "This is a strong concept, but the unit economics don't work at current volumes — here's how we fix that"
- The **stage-gate process** (pilot → regional → national) shows disciplined thinking
- Combine with STP (who to target first) and Marketing Mix (how to launch) for a complete recommendation
- Common closing: "To maximize this product's chance of success, I recommend a phased launch starting with the urban professional segment, with a clear break-even target of 3 Cr units in Year 2"
