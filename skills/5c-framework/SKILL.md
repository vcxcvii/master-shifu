# /5c-framework

## When to Use

Use this framework when you need:
- A comprehensive situational analysis before strategy development
- A structured way to organize market intelligence
- A pre-meeting preparation structure for client discussions
- A framework to ensure no critical external factor is overlooked
- A starting point for marketing strategy, growth strategy, or market entry

**Triggers**: "analyze the situation", "market analysis", "situational analysis", "where do we stand", "external environment".

### C1: Company

Analyze the client's internal situation:

**Performance**
- Revenue, profit, and growth trends (last 3-5 years)
- Market share and share trend
- Product/service portfolio: what's growing, what's declining?
- Customer metrics: retention, acquisition cost, lifetime value
- Brand health: awareness, perception, NPS

**Capabilities**
- Core competencies: what does the company do better than anyone?
- Technology and IP: patents, proprietary systems, R&D pipeline
- Operational capabilities: manufacturing, supply chain, distribution
- Talent: key people, organizational depth, culture

**Resources**
- Financial: cash, debt capacity, access to capital
- Physical: facilities, equipment, locations
- Relational: partnerships, customer relationships, supplier ties

**Strategic Position**
- Current strategy: what are they trying to do?
- Competitive advantage: cost, differentiation, focus, network effects?
- Strategic gaps: where are they underinvested or overexposed?

### C2: Customers

**Who are the customers?**
- Demographics: age, income, location, gender
- Psychographics: values, lifestyle, attitudes, interests
- Behavioral: usage frequency, purchase occasion, loyalty status
- Needs: functional needs, emotional needs, social needs

**What do they value?**
- Key purchase criteria: price, quality, convenience, service, brand
- Importance vs. performance: what matters most vs. how the client performs
- Willingness to pay: price sensitivity and elasticity

**How do they behave?**
- Purchase process: awareness → consideration → purchase → post-purchase
- Information sources: where do they research?
- Channel preference: online, retail, direct, B2B
- Switching costs: how hard is it to leave?

**Segment the customer base**
- Identify distinct segments with different needs and behaviors
- Size each segment (revenue and profit contribution)
- Growth trajectory of each segment
- Which segments are underpenetrated?

### C3: Competitors

**Identify Competitors**
- Direct competitors: same product, same customer, same geography
- Indirect competitors: substitute products meeting the same need
- Potential entrants: who could enter this market?

**Competitor Analysis**
- Market share and position (leader, challenger, follower, niche)
- Financial performance and resources
- Product/service offerings: features, quality, breadth
- Pricing strategy and cost structure
- Distribution and channel strategy
- Marketing and brand positioning
- Strengths and weaknesses vs. the client

**Competitive Dynamics**
- Recent moves: new products, pricing changes, M&A, partnerships
- Strategic intent: are they investing, defending, or retreating?
- Competitive response: how would they react to the client's moves?
- Industry rivalry: intensity of competition

### C4: Collaborators

**Suppliers**
- Key suppliers and concentration risk
- Supplier power and bargaining leverage
- Quality, reliability, and cost trends
- Opportunities for vertical integration or partnership

**Distributors & Channel Partners**
- Channel structure and margins
- Channel power: who controls access to customers?
- Partner incentives and alignment
- Opportunities for improved channel relationships

**Strategic Partners**
- Current partnerships: JVs, alliances, co-marketing
- Complementary businesses that could be partners
- Technology or content partners

**Other Stakeholders**
- Investors and analysts
- Industry associations
- Unions
- Community and advocacy groups
- Media and influencers

### C5: Context (PESTEL + Market Dynamics)

Analyze macro forces: Political (stability, regulation, trade policy), Economic (GDP, interest rates, inflation, consumer confidence), Social (demographics, lifestyle shifts, values), Technological (automation, AI, digital transformation, R&D), Environmental (climate, ESG, resource scarcity, carbon regulation), Legal (industry-specific compliance, labor laws). Also analyze Market Dynamics: size, growth rate, lifecycle stage, profitability trends, entry/exit barriers.

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

### Step 1: Start with Customer
Always start with the customer. Who are they, what do they need, and how are those needs changing? Strategy exists to serve customers. Understanding them first anchors the rest of the analysis.

### Step 2: Analyze Company vs. Competitors
How does the client compare to competitors in serving customer needs? Where do they win? Where are they losing? This identifies the competitive gap.

### Step 3: Understand Context
What external forces are shaping the market? Are tailwinds or headwinds dominant? Context explains why the industry is the way it is and where it's going.

### Step 4: Assess Collaborators
How well does the value chain work? Are suppliers, distributors, and partners aligned with the client's strategy? Are there collaboration opportunities?

### Step 5: Synthesize
Identify the key insights from each C. Cross-cut to find opportunities and threats.

## Example

**Scenario**: Premium bottled water company expanding into a new region.

**C1 – Company**: Strong brand, 95% awareness, 45% gross margin. Weak distribution in target region.

**C2 – Customers**: Health-conscious, urban, high-income. Value purity and status. Want sustainable packaging.

**C3 – Competitors**: Two players with 60% share. Local brand 30% cheaper. Exclusive shelf-space contracts.

**C4 – Collaborators**: Three distributors control 70% of retail. All locked in exclusive competitor deals.

**C5 – Context**: Premium hydration trending up. Plastic regulation tightening. Glass costs rising 8%/yr.

**Synthesis**: Growing demand for sustainable premium water is an opportunity, but competitors have locked distribution. Strategic implication: acquire a local distributor or build DTC / subscription channel.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It's Wrong | Correct Approach |
|---------|---------------|------------------|
| Starting with Company | Internal focus leads to missing market realities | Start with Customer—strategy serves the market |
| Listing facts without insight | A list of 50 facts is not analysis | For each C, identify the KEY insight |
| Treating Context as an afterthought | External factors often determine success or failure | Give Context its due weight—it shapes everything |
| Forgetting Collaborators | Value chain partners are critical to strategy | Include suppliers, distributors, and partners |
| Confusing competitors and collaborators | A competitor today could be a partner tomorrow | Stay open to coopetition |
| Static analysis | The 5Cs change over time | Consider trends, not just current state |
| Ignoring the customer's customer (in B2B) | B2B clients serve end-users too | Trace the full demand chain |
| Analysis without synthesis | 5C outputs are inputs to strategy, not the strategy itself | Always draw strategic implications from each C |
