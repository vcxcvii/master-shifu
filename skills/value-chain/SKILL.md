# Value Chain Analysis

## When to Use
- Understand a company's cost structure and margin drivers
- Identify sources of competitive advantage (cost or differentiation)
- Benchmark operations against competitors
- M&A due diligence — what capabilities are we buying?
- Identify opportunities for vertical integration or outsourcing
- Diagnose operational problems: where is value leaking?
- Improve profitability: reduce costs or increase willingness-to-pay

**Key insight**: Competitive advantage comes from either (1) performing activities at lower cost than competitors, or (2) performing activities in a way that justifies a premium price.

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1. **Map primary activities** — What does the company do, step by step?
   - **Inbound Logistics**: receiving, storing, distributing inputs. Key factors: supplier relationships, inventory systems, material handling
   - **Operations**: transforming inputs into outputs. Key factors: production technology, capacity, process efficiency, quality control
   - **Outbound Logistics**: collecting, storing, distributing products to customers. Key factors: delivery speed, order processing, warehouse locations
   - **Marketing & Sales**: making buyers aware and facilitating purchase. Key factors: pricing, promotion, sales force, channel management, branding
   - **Service**: maintaining value after purchase. Key factors: installation, repair, training, customer support, warranty

2. **Map support activities** — What enables the primary activities?
   - **Firm Infrastructure**: management, finance, legal, accounting — supports the entire chain
   - **HR Management**: recruiting, training, compensation — affects all activities' quality and cost
   - **Technology Development**: R&D, process improvement, IT — drives innovation and efficiency
   - **Procurement**: purchasing inputs — affects quality and cost of all other activities

3. **For each activity, assess:**
   - **Cost**: What does this activity cost? What % of total cost? Is it a large or small share?
   - **Value creation**: How important is this activity to the customer's willingness to pay? Would they notice if we did it worse/better?
   - **Benchmark**: How do we compare to competitors on cost AND quality for this activity?
   - **Differentiation potential**: Can we do this activity uniquely well to charge a premium?

4. **Identify competitive advantage**:
   - **Cost advantage**: Perform activities at lower cost (economies of scale, learning curve, process innovation, superior procurement, location advantages). Strategy: match competitor quality at lower cost.
   - **Differentiation**: Perform activities better (higher quality, faster, more features, better service, stronger brand). Strategy: justify a premium price through superior value.

5. **Analyze linkages** — This is where the real insight lives:
   - Linkages between activities: e.g., better procurement (better inputs) → less rework in operations (lower cost) → higher satisfaction in service (less returns)
   - Linkages with suppliers: e.g., JIT inventory reduces warehousing costs
   - Linkages with channels: e.g., training retailers improves in-store experience
   - Competitive advantage often comes from optimizing the SYSTEM of activities, not any single one

6. **Recommend** — Which activities to invest in, outsource, reengineer, or divest?

## Example

**Zara (Inditex) — Fast fashion value chain:**
| Activity | Industry standard | Zara |
|---|---|---|
| *Design* | 6 months lead time | 2 weeks from concept to store; 10K+ designs/year |
| *Manufacturing* | 100% outsourced to low-cost countries | 50% owned, near Spain — smaller batches, faster |
| *Logistics* | Sea freight (30-60 days) | Air freight + automated distribution (24-48 hrs) |
| *Retail* | Full seasons, markdown at end | Limited runs, scarcity → 85% full-price sell-through |
| *Data* | Quarterly sales reports | Real-time data from store managers → demand-driven production |

**Key insight**: Zara's advantage isn't any single activity — it's the *linkages* between design, production, logistics, and retail data that enable the 2-week cycle. Competitors can't replicate this because they'd need to change their entire value chain system.

**Cost advantage types found**: Process efficiency (small batches reduce inventory risk), automation in distribution, centralized design
**Differentiation types found**: Speed (new styles every 2 weeks), scarcity (limited runs), trend responsiveness

## Common Pitfalls
- Analyzing every activity equally — focus on the 2-3 that drive 80% of cost or value (Pareto principle)
- Ignoring linkages between activities — this is where competitive advantage truly lives
- Forgetting to benchmark against competitors — a "strength" is meaningless without comparison
- Treating the chain as static — activities and their importance evolve (e.g., digital channels changing marketing)
- Missing the customer's perspective — not every activity matters to the buyer; focus on those that do
- Analyzing the chain in isolation — suppliers and channels are part of the broader value system
- Confusing activities with departments — the value chain is about processes, not org structure
- Forgetting technology — technology development affects EVERY activity, not just R&D
