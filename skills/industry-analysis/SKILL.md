## When to Use

The client needs to understand the industry they operate in, evaluate whether to enter an industry, assess market attractiveness for investment, or understand competitive dynamics. Use as a standalone analysis or as a module within Business Situation (the Competition quadrant) or Market Entry frameworks.

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1. **Define the industry boundary**: What industry are we analyzing? Be precise — "the automotive industry" is too broad. "Premium EV sedans in North America" is actionable. Include upstream (suppliers), downstream (buyers), and adjacent (substitutes, potential entrants).

2. **Analyze each force**:

   **1. Threat of New Entrants** — How hard is it for a new competitor to enter?
   - Capital requirements: How much investment needed to compete?
   - Economies of scale: Do incumbents have a structural cost advantage?
   - Brand loyalty: How strong are existing brands? Switching costs for customers?
   - Regulatory barriers: Patents, licenses, tariffs, compliance costs
   - Network effects: Does value increase with more users?
   - Access to distribution: Can a new entrant get shelf space / dealer access?
   - Incumbent advantages: Proprietary technology, experience curve, access to raw materials

   If high barriers → incumbents protected. Low barriers → new entrants will compete away profits.

   **2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers** — Can suppliers charge more or reduce quality?
   - How many suppliers vs buyers in the industry?
   - Are there substitute inputs?
   - How important is the supplier's input to the buyer's business?
   - Do suppliers have differentiated or commodity products?
   - Can suppliers forward-integrate into the industry?
   - What are switching costs to change suppliers?

   High supplier power → input costs rise → industry profits compress.

   **3. Bargaining Power of Buyers** — Can customers demand lower prices?
   - How concentrated are buyers? (Few buyers with large purchase volume = high power)
   - Is the product standardized or differentiated?
   - What are switching costs for buyers?
   - Are buyers price sensitive?
   - Do buyers have information to negotiate effectively?
   - Can buyers backward-integrate (make instead of buy)?

   High buyer power → customers squeeze margins → lower industry profitability.

   **4. Threat of Substitutes** — What else can customers use instead?
   - Identify direct substitutes (same function, different form) and indirect
   - Compare price-performance tradeoff of substitute vs your product
   - What are switching costs to the substitute?
   - What is the buyer's propensity to substitute?

   Strong substitutes → industry can't charge high prices → lower profits.

   **5. Industry Rivalry** — How intense is competition among existing players?
   - Number and size of competitors (fragmented vs concentrated)
   - Industry growth rate (slow growth → more rivalry for share)
   - Product differentiation (commodity → price wars)
   - Exit barriers (high exit barriers → firms stay and fight)
   - Fixed costs (high fixed costs → pressure to fill capacity → price cuts)

   High rivalry → price competition, advertising wars → lower profits.

3. **Assess overall industry attractiveness**: Strong forces = unattractive industry (low average profitability). Weak forces = attractive industry (high average profitability). Not all forces are equal — identify the 1-2 strongest forces driving industry dynamics.

4. **Identify industry lifecycle stage**: Introduction (high risk, high potential) → Growth (rising demand, new entrants) → Maturity (consolidation, efficiency focus) → Decline (exit, cost focus). Each stage has different strategic priorities.

5. **Determine Key Success Factors**: What must a company do well to win in this industry? These flow from the 5 forces analysis. (E.g., in an industry with strong supplier power, backward integration may be a KSF.)

6. **Strategic implications**: Based on the analysis, what should the client do? Mitigate threats, leverage opportunities, position to maximize bargaining power.

## Example: EV Charging Industry Analysis

**Industry**: Level 2 and DC fast charging stations in the US.

**New Entrants**: TRES ALTA — capital requirements $50M+ for network deployment, permitting is complex (varies by municipality), equipment supply is constrained (few manufacturers). BUT government subsidies attract entrants. Incumbents (ChargePoint, EVgo, Tesla) have established site relationships.

**Supplier Power**: ALTA — charging equipment manufacturers (ABB, Siemens) are concentrated. Grid connection depends on local utilities (monopoly suppliers). Installation labor is scarce.

**Buyer Power**: MODERATE — EV drivers have apps to compare prices, switching costs are low (no loyalty programs yet). But charging is a necessity for EV owners.

**Substitutes**: MODERATE — home charging (Level 1/2) handles 80% of needs. Gasoline (for PHEV and ICE). Battery swapping. Wireless charging emerging.

**Rivalry**: INTENSE — low differentiation (charging is charging), high fixed costs (real estate, equipment), capacity utilization drives profitability. Price competition emerging.

**Overall**: Moderately attractive but shifting. High growth covers many sins today. As growth slows, rivalry will intensify, margins will compress. Key success factors: site location (high-traffic corridors), reliability (better uptime → stronger brand), and utility relationships.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Checklist without prioritization**: Listing all 5 forces without identifying which 1-2 are most impactful. Not all forces are equally strong in every industry. Focus on the dominant forces.
- **Ignoring complements**: Complements increase demand for your product (e.g., apps increase demand for smartphones). Porter's original framework underplays complements. Explicitly add them.
- **Static analysis in a dynamic industry**: Industries change. 5 forces is a snapshot. Add a trend arrow to each force — strengthening (more threatening) or weakening.
- **Too broad or narrow definition**: Defining the industry too broadly (e.g., "transportation") misses important competitive dynamics. Too narrowly misses substitutes and entrants.
- **Confusing industry with company**: An attractive industry doesn't mean every company in it is profitable. And an unattractive industry can have profitable niches (e.g., discount airlines vs premium airlines).
- **Forgetting government/regulation**: Government is a force that affects all 5 forces — as an entrant barrier (regulations), a buyer (Medicare/Medicaid), or a substitute shaper (subsidies for alternatives).
