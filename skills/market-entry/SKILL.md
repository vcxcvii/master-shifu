## When to Use

The client is considering entering a new geographic market, launching a product in a new customer segment, or expanding into a related industry. The core question is always: "Should we enter, and if so, how?"

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

1. **Define the market**: Geography? Customer segment? Product scope? Get specific — "enter India" is too vague; "enter urban Indian premium coffee segment via QSR partnerships" is actionable.

2. **Assess market attractiveness**:
   - Market size (TAM, SAM, SOM in $ and units)
   - Growth rate (is it worth the investment?)
   - Customer segments and needs — do they want what we sell?
   - Willingness to pay — can we price at our model?
   - Distribution landscape — how do products reach customers?
   - Regulatory environment — licenses, tariffs, restrictions
   - Cultural fit — does our positioning resonate?

3. **Assess company feasibility**:
   - Can we win? Compare our capabilities to what the market needs
   - Cost structure — can we compete on price/cost?
   - Brand awareness — do we have any? Need to build?
   - Management bandwidth — who runs the new market?
   - Investment required — can we fund it?

4. **Analyze competition**:
   - Who is there now? Market shares and positioning
   - How will they respond to our entry? Price war? Aggressive marketing?
   - Barriers to entry (capital, regulation, brand loyalty, scale)
   - Barriers to exit (if it fails, can we leave cheaply?)

5. **Entry mode decision**:
   - **Build organically**: Full control, slow, expensive, risky
   - **Acquire**: Quick, gains existing customers/talent, integration risk
   - **Partner/JV**: Shared risk, shared reward, control issues
   - **License/Franchise**: Low investment, low control

6. **Go/no-go recommendation** with rationale. If go → specific entry plan (timeline, investment, milestones). If no-go → alternative (different market, wait and prepare, don't enter).

## Example: Tesla entering Indian market

**Market attractiveness**: India EV market growing 40% YoY but from tiny base (~1.5% of auto sales). Price-sensitive market where the best-selling car costs $8-12K — Tesla's cheapest is $40K+ imported. Charging infrastructure is sparse.

**Company feasibility**: Tesla has brand cachet in tech/urban circles but no service network in India. Import duties are 60-100%. Building a factory requires large CapEx and years of regulatory process.

**Competition**: Tata Motors dominates domestic EVs at $15-25K. Hyundai/Kia mid-range. Ola Electric entering mass market. Incumbents would respond with price cuts.

**Recommendation**: Don't enter directly yet. Partner with a local OEM for CKD assembly to avoid 100% import duty, and invest in charging infrastructure to prime the market. Timing: wait 3-5 years until market reaches sufficient scale for a localized factory.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Ignoring cultural differences**: What works at home may fail abroad. Distribution norms, consumption habits, negotiation styles differ.
- **Underestimating incumbents**: Local players won't cede share quietly. Anticipate their response — price cuts, loyalty programs, regulatory pushback.
- **Assuming home advantages transfer**: Brand power, cost advantages, and operational excellence don't always travel.
- **Too much top-down, not enough bottom-up**: Big attractive market doesn't mean you can capture share. Ask: who will our first 100 customers be?
- **Ignoring capital requirements**: Many market entries fail not from bad strategy but from underfunding. You need enough runway to survive the learning curve.
- **One-size entry mode**: Build vs acquire vs partner depends on speed needed, risk appetite, capabilities gap, and integration complexity.
- **Not having an exit plan**: If entry fails, what's the cost of exiting? Leases, employee severance, brand damage, lost investment. Plan the exit before entering.
