# BCG Matrix / Growth-Share Matrix

## When to Use

The BCG Matrix is used when a client has multiple products, business units, or brands and needs to decide where to invest capital, which units to divest, and how to balance the portfolio for long-term growth. Typical triggers:

- Client asks: "Which of our products should we invest in?"
- "We have 5 business units — where should we allocate our Rs 50 Cr budget?"
- "Should we exit our low-growth product line?"
- Portfolio review, resource allocation, M&A prioritization
- Used early in case interviews to show structured portfolio thinking

## Framework Structure

The BCG Matrix plots business units on a 2x2 grid:

- **X-axis: Relative Market Share** (high → low) — measures competitive strength
- **Y-axis: Market Growth Rate** (high → low) — measures market attractiveness

Four quadrants:

### Stars (High Growth, High Share)
- Market leading in a fast-growing market
- Generate substantial revenue but need heavy investment to maintain position
- **Strategic goal:** Hold / Invest for growth
- **Cash flow:** Self-sustaining or slight negative
- **Examples:** Tesla in EVs (2018-2023), iPhone in early smartphone market

### Cash Cows (Low Growth, High Share)
- Market leader in a mature, slow-growing market
- Generate strong, predictable cash flow with minimal investment
- **Strategic goal:** Harvest — maximize cash extraction with minimal reinvestment
- **Cash flow:** Strongly positive — fund the rest of the portfolio
- **Examples:** Microsoft Office, Coca-Cola in developed markets

### Question Marks (High Growth, Low Share)
- Low share in a fast-growing market
- High cash demand (need to invest to keep up with growth) but low cash generation
- **Strategic goal:** Either invest heavily to become a Star, or divest
- **Cash flow:** Highly negative
- **Examples:** A new entrant in the EV charging market, a small player in cloud computing

### Dogs (Low Growth, Low Share)
- Low share in a stagnant or declining market
- Neither generate nor require much cash
- **Strategic goal:** Divest, harvest, or reposition
- **Cash flow:** Break-even or slight negative
- **Examples:** DVD rental stores in 2015, feature phones in 2020

## Step-by-Step Walkthrough

### Step 1: Define the relevant market for each business unit
- Don't define too narrowly (a product is always "high share" in a tiny market) or too broadly
- Example: For a premium chocolate brand, the market is "premium chocolate" not "all confectionery"

### Step 2: Calculate relative market share for each unit
- Formula: Your revenue ÷ Largest competitor's revenue
- Threshold: >1.0 is "high" share (you're the market leader)
- Alternative: Use market share percentage with 20%+ as "high"

### Step 3: Estimate market growth rate
- Use industry CAGR (past 3-5 years) or projected growth
- Threshold: Typically 10%+ is "high" growth, but varies by industry
- In interviews, the interviewer usually provides or confirms growth rates

### Step 4: Plot each unit on the grid
- Circle size = revenue or profit contribution (adds visual weight)
- This reveals the portfolio shape at a glance

### Step 5: Diagnose portfolio balance
- **Healthy portfolio:** Cash cows fund question marks that become stars → cows
- **Unhealthy:** Too many dogs (cash drain), too many question marks (spread thin), too few cows (no cash source)

### Step 6: Make strategic recommendations
- Stars → Invest for growth (maintain leadership)
- Cash Cows → Minimize reinvestment, maximize cash extraction
- Question Marks → Selectively invest in the most promising 1-2; divest the rest
- Dogs → Divest, harvest, or reposition into a niche

### Step 7: Validate with cash flow analysis
- Ensure total cash outflow (Stars + Question Marks) ≤ total cash inflow (Cash Cows + divestment proceeds)
- This is the "balance sheet" test of your portfolio strategy

## Example

**Context:** A diversified Indian conglomerate has 4 divisions:

| Division | Market Share | Market Growth | Revenue (Rs Cr) |
|----------|-------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Telecom | 15% (#3) | 20% YoY | 8,000 |
| Cement | 25% (#1) | 5% YoY | 12,000 |
| E-commerce | 5% (#5) | 30% YoY | 2,000 |
| Textiles | 8% (#4) | 2% YoY | 1,500 |

**Analysis:**
- **Cement:** Cash Cow (high share, low growth) — generates Rs 2,400 Cr EBITDA; minimize capex, use cash to fund other divisions
- **Telecom:** Question Mark (low share, high growth) — needs Rs 3,000 Cr investment; either commit fully or partner with a larger player
- **E-commerce:** Question Mark (very low share, very high growth) — assess if the company has the right capabilities; likely divest to a strategic buyer
- **Textiles:** Dog (low share, low growth) — divest and redeploy capital

**Recommendation:** Use cement cash flows to fund a focused bet on telecom (question mark → star). Divest textiles and e-commerce. This balances the portfolio and stops capital being spread across too many question marks.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Defining markets too narrowly or too broadly** — A "high share" product can look like a Star or a Dog depending on market definition. Always clarify the market boundary with the interviewer.

2. **Equating market share with profitability** — High share doesn't always mean high profit (e.g., a Star might be investing heavily in R&D and have thin margins). BCG Matrix is a cash-flow framework, not a profit framework.

3. **Treating Dogs as always bad** — Some Dogs generate reliable cash flow with zero investment. A "Dog" that generates Rs 100 Cr with no capex is better than a "Question Mark" that burns Rs 500 Cr. Evaluate cash flow, not just quadrant.

4. **Forgetting the dynamic nature** — Products move through the matrix over time (Question Mark → Star → Cash Cow → Dog). Don't recommend static solutions. Consider the trajectory.

5. **Ignoring synergies between units** — A Dog that provides critical data or distribution to a Star shouldn't be divested blindly. Ask about inter-unit dependencies.

6. **Using it as the only framework** — BCG Matrix tells you where to invest but not *how* to compete. Combine with Porter's Generic Strategies or Strategy Diamond for the full picture.

7. **Assuming high-growth markets are always attractive** — High growth attracts competitors, which may erode margins. Consider entry barriers and competitive intensity.

## Interview Tips

- Always draw the 2x2 grid explicitly on the whiteboard — it shows structured thinking
- Label axes correctly: Relative Market Share (not just "Market Share")
- Use circle sizes to represent revenue — this visual is distinctive and scores points
- Lead with the portfolio balance diagnosis ("You have 3 Question Marks and 1 Cash Cow — this is an unbalanced portfolio")
- Transition naturally into specific recommendations per quadrant
